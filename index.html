Human‑Centred Design for an ADHD‑Friendly Healthcare Journey

\n

Co‑designing practical solutions with adults with ADHD and Dutch healthcare staff.

\n
\n\n
\n

About the Project

\n

Healthcare encounters in the Netherlands are typically structured for neurotypical cognitive styles—predictable schedules, information‑dense consultations and sensory‑neutral spaces. Adults with Attention‑Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) often face friction at every touchpoint: booking and remembering appointments, sensory overload in waiting rooms and consultation formats that overwhelm executive functioning.

\n

Through ADHD Eyes harnesses a four‑phase human‑centred design (HCD) process to uncover these pain‑points alongside the people who experience them and to prototype low‑cost, real‑world fixes that make everyday healthcare more neuroaffirming.

\n
\n\n
\n

Study Aim

\n

The study engages adults with ADHD and frontline healthcare staff in a 16‑week design cycle that:

\n
    \n
  • Discovers friction points across three journey moments—Access & Booking, Waiting‑Room Experience and Consultation & Follow‑up.
    • \n
  • Generates “How‑Might‑We” opportunity statements and concept ideas.
    • \n
  • Builds and tests at least three prototype service modifications, such as sensory zoning kits and ADHD‑friendly appointment reminders.
    • \n
\n

Our ultimate objective is to produce an open‑access ADHD‑Friendly Clinic Starter Pack that can be rolled out across Dutch primary‑care settings.

\n
\n\n
\n

Study Method

\n

The protocol follows IDEO’s Human‑Centred Design framework:

\n
    \n
  1. Inspiration (Weeks 1–4) – Observations of booking flows and waiting areas, plus rapid post‑consultation interviews to surface lived‑experience insights.
    2. \n
  2. Ideation (Weeks 5–6) – Stakeholder workshops using brain‑writing and sketching techniques to generate and prioritise solutions.
    3. \n
  3. Prototyping (Weeks 7–11) – Co‑creating paper or digital models of the top concepts and trialling them in a partner clinic over two iterative cycles.
    4. \n
  4. Implementation & Spread (Weeks 12–16) – Refining successful prototypes and compiling the Starter Pack and travelling exhibition for wider dissemination.
    5. \n
\n

The focus is on service‑improvement artefacts rather than clinical data, so the study sits outside the scope of the Dutch Medical Research Involving Human Subjects Act.
