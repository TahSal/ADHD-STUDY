Human‑Centred Design for an ADHD‑Friendly Healthcare Journey

Co‑designing practical solutions with adults with ADHD and Dutch healthcare staff.

About the Project

Healthcare encounters in the Netherlands are traditionally optimised for neurotypical cognitive styles—tight schedules, information‑dense consultations and sensory‑neutral spaces. Adults with Attention‑Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) often hit friction at every touch‑point: booking and remembering appointments, sensory overload in waiting rooms and consultation formats that overwhelm executive functioning.

Through ADHD Eyes applies a four‑phase human‑centred design (HCD) approach to surface these pain‑points alongside those who experience them and to co‑create low‑cost, real‑world adjustments that make everyday healthcare more neuro‑affirming.

Study Aim

The 16‑week study brings together adults with ADHD and frontline healthcare professionals to:

  • Map friction points across three journey moments—Access & Booking, Waiting‑Room Experience and Consultation & Follow‑up.
  • Formulate “How‑Might‑We” opportunity statements and sketch solution concepts.
  • Prototype and test at least three service modifications such as sensory zoning kits and ADHD‑friendly appointment reminders.

Our end‑goal is an open‑access ADHD‑Friendly Clinic Starter Pack ready for roll‑out across Dutch primary care.

Study Method

The protocol follows IDEO’s Human‑Centred Design rhythm:

  1. Inspiration (Weeks 1–4) – Contextual enquiry of booking flows and waiting areas, plus rapid post‑consultation interviews to surface lived‑experience insights.
  2. Ideation (Weeks 5–6) – Stakeholder workshops using brain‑writing and sketching to generate and prioritise ideas.
  3. Prototyping (Weeks 7–11) – Co‑creating paper or digital models of the top concepts and trialling them in a partner clinic over two iterative cycles.
  4. Implementation & Spread (Weeks 12–16) – Refining successful prototypes, packaging them into the Starter Pack and sharing via a travelling exhibition.

The project centres on service‑improvement artefacts rather than clinical interventions, so it falls outside the Dutch Medical Research Involving Human Subjects Act.
